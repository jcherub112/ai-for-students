A PRACTICAL GUIDE FOR STUDENTS

Use AI to learn more, faster — without cheating yourself.

Your professors will have opinions. Employers will have expectations. This site gives you the short version of what actually works.

Dr. Jason Cherubini · Executive-in-Residence / Assistant Teaching Professor · Sellinger School of Business · Loyola University Maryland
TL;DR

AI is the new Excel. You need it — but you need to use it in a way that makes you smarter, not lazier. This site gives you five things: tools, prompts, course-specific playbooks, integrity rules, and a searchable library.

Start with Prompting 101. Then pick your course type. Then steal from the Prompt Library.

Why you need this

Research from Anthropic (March 2026) shows that roughly 85% of business and finance tasks can be meaningfully assisted by AI. The gap between what's possible and what's actually being used is closing fast. By the time you graduate, employers will assume you're fluent.

At the same time: most students use AI badly. They paste the assignment in, copy the answer out, and learn nothing. Their grades are fine until the exam. Then they're not.

This site is the middle path: use AI as a scaffold, not a shortcut.

Tools

Which AI should I use?

TL;DR

For most schoolwork: Claude or ChatGPT. Pick one and learn it well.

Use NotebookLM when you have PDFs or lecture slides. Use Gemini if you live in Google Docs. Use Copilot if your school gave you an M365 account.

The short list

ToolBest ForFree Tier?
ChatGPTGeneral schoolwork, brainstorming, code, broad knowledge. Huge ecosystem of Custom GPTs.Yes (limited GPT-4 usage)
ClaudeLong documents, nuanced analysis, writing feedback, research. Best reasoning for finance and econ.Yes
GeminiAnything connected to Google Docs, Gmail, Drive. Good free tier.Yes
NotebookLMUploading a pile of PDFs, lecture slides, or a textbook and asking grounded questions. Creates audio "podcast" summaries.Yes
CopilotInside Word, Excel, PowerPoint — if you have the right M365 license.Limited

Which one should I actually download?

Pick one chat tool (Claude or ChatGPT) and learn its quirks. Then add NotebookLM for any class with lots of reading. That's enough for 90% of students.

Student Pro Tip Claude and ChatGPT both have free tiers that are genuinely good for most school tasks. Don't pay for Plus until you actually hit the limits. If you do pay, it's $20/month — cheaper than a textbook and more useful than most of them.

Features you should actually use

1. Custom GPTs / Projects / Gems

All three major tools let you create a custom workspace where you upload your syllabus, lecture notes, and textbook PDFs. The AI then answers your questions using your course materials, not its generic training data. This is a massive upgrade over plain chatting. See the Study Playbooks.

2. Voice mode

Talking to AI is faster than typing. Use voice mode to quiz yourself before an exam, practice a presentation, or explain a concept back to it and see if your explanation holds up.

3. File uploads

Drop your rubric, the professor's slides, and your draft all in one conversation. The AI will actually use them instead of guessing.

Trap: Don't trust citations AI tools — especially ChatGPT and Claude — will invent sources that don't exist. They'll give you fake authors, fake page numbers, even fake DOIs. Never cite something an AI gave you without verifying it yourself. Use Google Scholar, your library, or NotebookLM (which only cites uploaded sources).
Try This Now Go to Claude.ai or ChatGPT.com. Start a new chat. Paste your course syllabus. Ask: "Based on this syllabus, what are the three hardest weeks for me to prepare for, and what should I do each week to stay ahead?"
Course-Specific Playbooks

Use AI in your specific kind of course

TL;DR

Every discipline has a "right" way to use AI that doesn't hurt your learning. Pick your course type below and steal the prompts.

Finance & Accounting

Use AI for: explaining concepts before the professor does, walking through problem sets step-by-step, interpreting financial statements, practicing exam problems with instant feedback.

Don't use AI for: doing the calculations for you without you checking them. AI still makes arithmetic mistakes, especially in time-value-of-money and DCF problems.

  • Concept check: "Explain the difference between WACC and cost of equity like I'm a sophomore who just took intro finance. Give one numerical example."
  • Problem walkthrough: "Here's a problem from my textbook: [paste]. Walk me through it step by step. At each step, pause and ask me what I'd do next before telling me the answer."
  • Exam prep: "Generate 5 exam-style questions on CAPM, beta, and the SML. Give me the questions first. I'll answer them. Then grade me and tell me what to review."

Business & Management (Strategy, Marketing, OB)

Use AI for: brainstorming frameworks (Porter's, SWOT, 4Ps), stress-testing your thesis, generating counter-arguments, writing rough outlines for case discussions.

Don't use AI for: writing the case analysis itself. Professors are excellent at spotting AI-written case analyses — they're always vague in the same ways.

STEM (Bio, Chem, Physics, Engineering)

Use AI for: re-explaining textbook passages, generating analogies, walking through derivations, debugging code, helping you study for the conceptual parts of exams.

Don't use AI for: solving numerical problems you haven't tried yourself. And always verify — AI still hallucinates chemistry and physics facts.

Humanities (History, Philosophy, English)

Use AI for: summarizing dense readings before class, stress-testing your arguments, generating opposing viewpoints, proofreading, helping you brainstorm thesis statements.

Don't use AI for: writing the essay. Humanities essays are about your thinking. AI-written essays are generic, thesis-free, and your professor has read thousands of them.

Writing-Intensive Courses

Use AI for: feedback on your drafts (huge), reverse-outlining what you already wrote, catching repetition, suggesting stronger verbs, simulating a peer reviewer.

Try this prompt: "Here's my draft. Don't rewrite it. Tell me: (1) what my thesis actually is based on what I wrote, (2) where my argument is weakest, (3) what a skeptical reader would push back on."

Quantitative Courses (Stats, Data, Econometrics)

Use AI for: explaining what a regression output actually means, helping you code in R/Python/Stata, debugging errors, translating textbook notation into plain English.

Don't use AI for: interpreting your data without you looking at it yourself. AI will confidently misread a p-value or a coefficient sign.

Try This Now Pick one class where you feel behind. Open Claude or ChatGPT. Paste this: "I'm in [COURSE]. We just covered [TOPIC]. I'm supposed to understand [SPECIFIC CONCEPT]. Explain it three different ways: (1) plain English, (2) an analogy, (3) a worked example. Then ask me a question to check if I got it."
Core Skill

Prompting 101: The 4-Part Formula

TL;DR

A good prompt has four parts: Role, Task, Context, Format. Get these right and AI stops sounding generic.

Bad prompt: "Explain marginal cost". Good prompt: "You're a patient econ tutor. Explain marginal cost to me — I'm in intro micro at Loyola, I understand demand curves but I'm lost on derivatives. Use a pizza example. Keep it under 150 words, then ask me a check question."

The formula

PartWhat it isExample
RoleWho the AI should act as"You're a patient finance tutor"
TaskWhat you want it to do"Quiz me on CAPM"
ContextWhat you know, what you don't, what class this is for"I'm in intro finance. I understand beta but not how to read the SML"
FormatHow you want the answer (length, structure, tone)"Ask one question at a time. Wait for my answer. Then grade me."

Before/After

❌ Before

Explain the Black-Scholes formula

You'll get a Wikipedia-style dump. Useless.

✅ After

You're a patient finance tutor for undergrads. I'm in an intro options class. I understand the idea of a call option (right to buy at a strike price) but the Black-Scholes formula looks like a wall of Greek letters to me. Explain the formula in three stages: 1. What problem it solves, in plain English (no math) 2. What each variable means, with a concrete example from a real stock 3. One gotcha students always miss Then give me one short problem I could work out on paper. Don't give me the answer — I'll come back with it.

You'll get something you can actually learn from.

Five moves that make prompts dramatically better

  1. "Ask me questions first." Instead of dumping a task, tell the AI to interview you before it answers. It makes the output 10x more relevant.
  2. "Don't give me the answer." Tell it to walk you through, pause, wait, and let you try. This is the difference between learning and copying.
  3. "Push back on me." Tell the AI to be skeptical of your reasoning. Default AI behavior is to agree with you, which is useless for learning.
  4. Paste the rubric. If you have one, paste it. AI will grade itself against it.
  5. "Keep it under X words." Forces it to be concrete and stops the bullet-point sprawl.
Trap: The "do my homework" prompt "Write my essay on Hamlet" produces a bad essay that your professor will spot in 10 seconds. "Interview me for 15 minutes about Hamlet, then help me turn my own ideas into a thesis statement" produces an essay that's actually yours — and better.

A prompt you can steal right now

You are a [SUBJECT] tutor for college undergrads. I'm preparing for [EXAM/ASSIGNMENT]. Here's what I know: - [LIST WHAT YOU ACTUALLY UNDERSTAND] Here's what I don't: - [LIST WHAT YOU'RE LOST ON] Do three things: 1. Ask me 3 quick diagnostic questions to check what I really know vs. what I think I know 2. Based on my answers, tell me the ONE concept I should focus on first and why 3. Teach me that concept with a concrete example, then quiz me Rule: never give me an answer before I've tried. Wait for my response.

The Rules

Academic Integrity & AI

TL;DR

Rule #1: Read your syllabus. Every professor's policy is different. A prompt that's fine in one class will get you suspended in another.

Rule #2: When in doubt, ask. A one-sentence email to your professor is cheaper than a Code of Conduct hearing.

Rule #3: Using AI to learn is almost never cheating. Using AI to replace your thinking almost always is.

The honest version

Higher ed is still figuring out AI policy. In 2026, at most universities including Loyola, the rules look roughly like this:

What you're doingUsually OK?
Asking AI to explain a concept you don't understand✅ Almost always
Asking AI to quiz you before an exam✅ Almost always
Asking AI to summarize a reading so you can decide where to focus✅ Usually
Asking AI to proofread or give feedback on a draft you wrote✅ Usually (but check)
Asking AI to generate an outline, which you then write from⚠️ Depends on the class
Asking AI to write a first draft you then edit⚠️ Often not OK without disclosure
Submitting AI-generated work as your own❌ Always cheating
Using AI on an exam that says "no AI"❌ Always cheating
Having AI solve your problem set and copying the answers❌ Always cheating, always detectable in the exam

How to ask your professor

Send this email the first week of the semester. Most professors will answer within 48 hours and appreciate you asking.

Subject: Question about AI use in [COURSE CODE] Dear Professor [NAME], I want to make sure I stay well inside your AI policy for the semester. Could I check a few specific uses with you? 1. Can I use AI to explain concepts I don't understand or to quiz myself before exams? 2. Can I use AI to get feedback on a draft I wrote, as long as I don't ask it to rewrite the draft itself? 3. For the [SPECIFIC ASSIGNMENT], what kinds of AI use are allowed or disallowed? 4. If I use AI in an allowed way, do you want me to disclose how I used it? Thank you for clarifying — I'd rather ask now than guess wrong. Best, [YOUR NAME] [COURSE / SECTION]
Trap: "Nobody will know" AI detectors are unreliable — but professors are not. They've read hundreds of AI-written essays and they recognize the patterns: the vague theses, the hedge words, the lists of three, the same exact sentence structures. Worse: if you didn't actually understand the material, you'll bomb the exam. The exam is the real detector.
The disclosure habit Get in the habit of adding a one-line note at the end of any assignment where you used AI: "I used Claude to quiz me on Chapter 4 and to give feedback on my draft. I did not use AI to write any portion of the submitted text." Most professors will respect this. Some will require it. None will penalize it.

What to do if you're accused

If a professor accuses you of inappropriate AI use and you're innocent:

  1. Don't panic and don't confess to something you didn't do.
  2. Show your version history. Google Docs and Word both track this. Drafts with slow progress, typos, and revisions look very different from AI output pasted in.
  3. Offer to explain your paper in a meeting. If you wrote it, you can talk about it.
  4. Contact Loyola's Honor Council for procedural guidance.
Try This Now Open your three hardest classes' syllabi. Search for "AI" or "ChatGPT" or "generative." Write down each professor's rule in one sentence. Tape it to the top of your notebook.
Playbooks

Study Playbooks: Step-by-step

TL;DR

Four playbooks: exam prep, paper writing, problem sets, and group projects. Each is a sequence of prompts you run in order.

Playbook 1: Exam Prep (5-day plan)

Day 1 — Diagnose

You're a study coach. I have an exam in [COURSE] on [DATE] covering [TOPICS]. Interview me: ask me one question at a time about each topic. Rate my answer on a 1-5 scale of understanding. Don't teach me yet — just diagnose. After 10 questions, give me: - A ranked list of topics from weakest to strongest - The ONE topic I should spend most of my time on - An estimated number of hours per topic based on my gaps

Day 2–3 — Teach

For [WEAKEST TOPIC from Day 1], teach me in three passes: 1. Plain English explanation (no jargon, <200 words) 2. A worked example with real numbers 3. A common mistake students make on exam questions about this Then give me ONE practice problem. Don't show me the answer until I try.

Day 4 — Practice Test

Generate a 10-question practice test on [TOPICS], weighted toward my weak areas. Mix question types: 3 conceptual, 4 calculation, 3 application. Give me the test first. I'll answer all 10. Then grade me and explain every wrong answer.

Day 5 — Final check

Quiz me rapid-fire on the 10 most likely exam questions based on the topics [LIST]. One question at a time. Mark my answers. After 10 questions, tell me if I'm ready or what to review tonight.

Playbook 2: Writing a Paper

  1. Brainstorm (you + AI): "I have to write a [X]-page paper on [TOPIC] for [COURSE]. Ask me 5 questions to help me find an angle I actually care about."
  2. Thesis draft (you alone first): Write your own thesis in one sentence before showing AI anything. Then: "Here's my thesis: [...]. Push back. Where is it weak? What would a skeptic say?"
  3. Outline (you + AI): "Based on my revised thesis, suggest a 5-section outline. For each section, tell me what question it answers."
  4. Draft (you alone): Write the first draft yourself. No AI here. This is where learning happens.
  5. Feedback (AI): "Here's my draft. Don't rewrite it. Tell me: (1) what my actual thesis is, based on what I wrote, (2) my weakest paragraph and why, (3) one concrete way to make paragraph X stronger."
  6. Revise (you alone). Apply the feedback in your own words.
  7. Proofread (AI): "Find typos, awkward sentences, and inconsistent verb tense. Do not change my argument or voice. Give me a list."

Playbook 3: Problem Sets

The golden rule: try it yourself first, then use AI to check and teach. If you skip step 1, you'll fail the exam, guaranteed.

  1. Attempt every problem yourself. Get as far as you can, even if you're stuck.
  2. For problems you couldn't solve: "Here's the problem: [paste]. Here's where I got stuck: [explain]. Don't give me the answer. Give me the NEXT step and explain why that step."
  3. For problems you finished: "Here's my answer: [paste]. Is it right? If not, where did I go wrong and why?"
  4. After the whole set: "Looking at the problems I struggled with, what's the underlying concept I'm missing? Teach me that concept in 150 words."

Playbook 4: Group Projects

  1. Kickoff: Paste the assignment and rubric. Ask AI to extract a checklist of deliverables with estimated hours per task.
  2. Roles: "Based on the deliverables, suggest 3-4 role assignments for a team of [N]. What's the handoff between roles?"
  3. Feedback rounds: Before submitting, run your team's draft through: "You're the grader using this rubric [paste]. Grade us harshly. Tell us the three specific things that would move us from a B+ to an A."

Level Up

Build Your Own Custom GPT (or Gem)

TL;DR

A Custom GPT is a private AI trained on your syllabus, notes, slides, and textbook — not the whole internet. It stops hallucinating your course and starts actually helping you learn it.

You can build one in under 30 minutes. You only need a free ChatGPT or Gemini account to use one someone shared with you. Building and keeping one private requires ChatGPT Plus ($20/mo) for GPTs, or Gemini Advanced for Gems.

Why bother?

When you chat with plain ChatGPT, it's guessing what your class covers. It doesn't know your professor's definitions, your textbook's chapter order, or the specific notation in your lecture notes. A Custom GPT fixes that. Upload your materials once, and every conversation from then on is grounded in your course.

Three kinds of Custom GPTs students should build:

  1. Course-specific tutor — one GPT per hard class (e.g., "FI320 Tutor"). Upload the syllabus, slides, and textbook. Use it for quizzing and concept checks.
  2. Personal study coach — one GPT that knows you. Your major, your goals, how you like to learn, what you're weak at. Use it for planning and feedback.
  3. Career GPT — one GPT that has your resume, target roles, and a list of companies. Use it for networking, interview prep, and application drafting.

ChatGPT: Build a Custom GPT

Step 1 — Open the builder

In ChatGPT (Plus or higher): sidebar → Explore GPTs → + Create. You'll see a two-panel layout: chat-based builder on the left, preview on the right.

Step 2 — Give it a clear job

Don't describe the GPT to itself. Instruct it. Paste something like this into the Configure tab's "Instructions" field:

You are a tutor for [COURSE NAME] at Loyola University Maryland. I am a [YEAR] undergraduate majoring in [MAJOR]. Your job is to help me learn, not to do my work for me. Rules you must follow, every conversation: 1. Ground every answer in the uploaded course materials (syllabus, slides, textbook). If the answer isn't in those materials, say so before giving a general answer. 2. Never give me the final answer to a problem until I've tried it. If I ask for help, ask me what I've tried first. 3. Quiz me using questions written in the style of my professor, based on the syllabus and slides. 4. Use the same notation and terminology as my textbook. 5. When I'm clearly confused, slow down. Re-explain in plainer English. 6. End every teaching session with ONE question to check my understanding. Tone: patient, direct, no filler. No emojis. No hedging.

Step 3 — Upload your materials

In the Configure tab, scroll to Knowledge and drop in:

  • Syllabus (PDF)
  • Lecture slides, in order, labeled by week
  • Any textbook chapters you have legally (scanned is fine)
  • Past problem sets with solutions
  • Practice exams the professor has shared

Do NOT upload: anything marked confidential, anything under a paywall you don't own, or anything that violates your course policy. Read the Academic Integrity page before you upload.

Step 4 — Test it

In the preview panel, ask a question you already know the answer to. If the GPT cites the wrong chapter, fix the Instructions. Ask a question from outside your syllabus — it should say "that's not in the course materials" before answering.

Step 5 — Save as "Only me"

When you click Save, choose Only me unless you're sharing with a study group. Anything on your syllabus that's not your original work should stay private for copyright reasons.

Gemini: Build a Gem

Google's version of Custom GPTs is called Gems. Open Gemini → sidebar → Gems → New Gem. The interface is simpler: a name, an instruction box, and a knowledge upload. Use the same instruction template as above. Gems work well if you already live in Google Docs and Drive, because they can read from your Drive directly.

Claude: Use Projects

Claude doesn't have "Custom Claudes" — it has Projects. Same idea: a workspace with uploaded knowledge and custom instructions. Create one at claude.ai → Projects → New Project. Paste the same instructions, upload your materials, and you're done. Claude Projects are included in the free tier with limits; Claude Pro is $20/mo.

Three starter Custom GPT ideas

Course Tutor

My [Course] Study Buddy

Upload: syllabus, slides, textbook. Use for: concept review, quizzing, problem walkthroughs, exam prep.

Writing Coach

My Writing Feedback GPT

Upload: a style guide (APA, Chicago), the rubric for your class, 2-3 of your graded papers. Use for: draft feedback in your voice.

Career

My Career Coach GPT

Upload: your resume, target job descriptions, LinkedIn bio. Use for: cover letters, interview prep, networking emails that sound like you.

Trap: Uploading copyrighted textbooks It's a grey area. If you own the PDF, uploading it to your own private GPT for personal study is generally considered fair use. Uploading it to a shared GPT and distributing it to classmates is not. If you're unsure, upload your own notes from the textbook instead of the textbook itself.
Pro move: iterate on the instructions The first version of your Custom GPT will be mediocre. That's normal. After using it for a week, note where it's annoying or wrong, go back to the Configure tab, and refine the instructions. Your third version will be dramatically better than your first.

Advanced Tool

Claude Cowork: Your AI Research Assistant

TL;DR

Cowork is a mode in the Claude desktop app where you describe an outcome — "organize these PDFs," "build me a study guide from these slides," "turn this data into a chart" — and Claude just does it. Multiple steps. Real files. Actual work.

It's like having a free research assistant who never sleeps. You need Claude Pro ($20/mo) to use it.

How Cowork is different from regular ChatGPT/Claude

Regular AI chat: you ask a question, it gives you text. You copy the text. You paste it somewhere. You ask the next question.

Cowork: you describe a goal, and Claude plans the steps, reads your files, runs code, creates documents, and hands you the finished work. You step away and come back when it's done.

Install it

  1. Go to claude.ai/download and install the desktop app for Mac or Windows.
  2. Sign in with your Claude account. Cowork requires a paid plan — Pro ($20/mo) is enough for students. Claude for Education is free at some schools; check with your IT.
  3. In the desktop app, look at the top of the window for a Chat / Cowork toggle. Click Cowork.
  4. When Cowork asks for a folder, point it at a working folder you control (e.g., a new folder on your desktop called cowork-scratch). Cowork reads and writes files there.
Trap: folder permissions Never point Cowork at your entire Downloads folder, your Documents folder, or anywhere with sensitive files (tax docs, saved logins, etc.). Create a dedicated folder for it. You can always move files in and out.

Five things to actually do with Cowork as a student

1. Build a study guide from a pile of slides

Drop all your lecture slides for the semester into the folder. Then tell Cowork:

Read every PDF in this folder. These are lecture slides for [COURSE] from Week 1 through Week [N]. Build me a study guide organized by week, with: - Each week's main topic - The 5 most important terms and their definitions from THAT week's slides - One practice question per week, written in the style of a final exam Save it as a Word document called Study_Guide.docx.

2. Turn a messy dataset into a chart

Got a CSV from a project or internship and no idea what's in it? Drop it in the folder and say:

Explore the file [filename.csv] in this folder. Tell me: 1. What columns it has and what kind of data each one holds 2. Any data quality issues (missing values, weird formats) 3. Three charts that would show the most interesting patterns in this data Then create those three charts as PNG files and save them to this folder.

3. Organize your writing course portfolio

Paste a dozen draft essays into a folder and:

Read every .docx file in this folder. For each one, create a one-line summary and a letter grade (A/B/C/D) based on clarity, argument strength, and evidence use. Save the results as a Markdown table in a file called portfolio_overview.md. Do not rewrite any of my essays — just analyze them.

4. Build a presentation from your notes

Read my notes in the file my_notes.md. I have to present for 8 minutes in [COURSE] on [TOPIC]. Build me a 10-slide PowerPoint called presentation.pptx with: - A title slide - 7 content slides covering the main ideas from my notes, one idea per slide - A summary slide - A Q&A slide Keep bullet points short (max 6 words). Don't make up any facts I didn't write.

5. Prep for an interview or case competition

Read the company_info.pdf in this folder (their 10-K). Build me an interview prep brief saved as interview_prep.docx that includes: 1. A 1-paragraph company summary 2. The three biggest risks they flagged 3. Five smart questions I could ask in an interview 4. Three behavioral questions THEY might ask me, with space under each for me to draft an answer

Cowork etiquette for students

  • Never give it your professor's unreleased exam. Obvious reasons.
  • Always review the files it creates before you submit anything. Cowork can make up facts the same way regular AI can.
  • Don't upload anyone else's private work — roommate's paper, group project files your team hasn't approved for AI use, etc.
  • Close the app when you're done. It runs in the background.
Student Pro Tip Cowork can schedule repeating tasks on Pro and higher. Set up one that every Sunday night reads your updated lecture slides folder and generates a fresh "What's coming this week" summary in Markdown. Then you wake up Monday already prepped.

Research Skills

Using AI as a Research Assistant

TL;DR

AI is excellent at starting a research project: scoping questions, mapping a literature, critiquing your methodology, catching logical gaps. It is terrible at finishing one: inventing citations, getting statistics wrong, and missing disciplinary judgment.

Use it for the 80% you can verify. Do the other 20% yourself.

What AI is good at (and what you should use it for)

Research taskCan AI help?Your job
Scoping a research question✅ ExcellentProvide context; pick the angle that matters to you
Explaining a methodology you don't know✅ ExcellentVerify against one real source before trusting
Critiquing your draft methodology✅ ExcellentDecide which critiques apply to your case
Scanning a literature (what's been done?)⚠️ Okay for scopingActually verify every single citation. AI hallucinates.
Finding specific citations❌ DangerousUse Google Scholar, your library, or NotebookLM instead
Running statistical analyses⚠️ Good with oversightAlways check the code and the output yourself
Writing a literature review⚠️ Rough first draft onlyRewrite in your voice; verify every claim
Interpreting your results⚠️ HelpfulThe interpretation must be yours — that's the research
Responding to reviewer/professor feedback✅ ExcellentDraft responses, then decide which to push back on
The #1 trap: hallucinated citations Every single chat AI (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) will invent academic sources that look real. Plausible authors. Real-sounding journals. Fake DOIs. If you cite a hallucinated source in a paper, your professor will find it (they Google things), and it's an integrity violation even if you didn't mean it. Rule: if you didn't click through to the real paper yourself, don't cite it.

A research workflow that actually works

Step 1: Scope your question (AI)

I'm writing a research paper for [COURSE] on [BROAD TOPIC]. I have [N] pages and [WEEKS] to finish. Ask me 6 questions — one at a time — to narrow this down to a specific, researchable question. After my answers, propose three possible research questions, ranked by: (a) how feasible it is in my time frame, (b) how interesting the finding will be, and (c) whether data or sources actually exist to answer it.

Step 2: Map the literature (NotebookLM is better than ChatGPT here)

For finding real sources, skip ChatGPT. Instead:

  • Google Scholar — search with your best keywords. Use the "Cited by" feature to jump forward in time from a seminal paper.
  • Your university library's databases — Business Source Complete, JSTOR, ProQuest. They only return real papers.
  • NotebookLM — once you've downloaded 10–15 real papers, upload them all to a NotebookLM notebook. It can only cite the sources you uploaded. No hallucinations. You can now ask "what do these papers agree on?" and get grounded answers with inline citations.
[In NotebookLM, after uploading your PDFs] I'm writing a research paper on [RESEARCH QUESTION]. Based ONLY on the sources in this notebook: 1. What are the main schools of thought or debates? 2. Which source makes the strongest argument for each side? 3. What's the most common methodology used in this area? 4. What's a gap in the literature I could contribute to in a [N]-page paper? Use direct citations to the uploaded sources.

Step 3: Design your methodology (AI is great here)

My research question is: [QUESTION]. I'm considering using [METHOD — e.g., regression, survey, case study, content analysis]. Do three things: 1. Explain this method in plain English as if to an undergrad 2. Tell me the three biggest threats to validity for this method in MY specific case 3. List the robustness checks or controls I should include 4. Tell me one common mistake students make when they use this method

Step 4: Run the analysis (carefully)

If your research involves data, you can use Cowork or ChatGPT's code interpreter to run analyses. Rules:

  • Always look at the code before you trust the output.
  • Run a quick sanity check — does the sample size make sense? Do means look reasonable?
  • If the AI says "the result is statistically significant," go read the p-value yourself.
  • Never report a number you can't explain where it came from.

Step 5: Draft (you) → critique (AI) → revise (you)

Write the draft yourself. Seriously — this is where the learning lives. Then use AI as a skeptical reviewer:

Here's my draft. Don't rewrite it. Act as Reviewer 2 — be tough but fair. Tell me: 1. What my actual argument is, in one sentence, based on what I wrote 2. The weakest claim in the paper and why 3. The most obvious counter-argument I failed to address 4. One specific place where my evidence doesn't support my claim 5. Anything that sounds hedged or vague DRAFT: [PASTE]

Disclosing AI use in research

A growing number of journals and professors require you to disclose how you used AI. Even when it's not required, disclosing is a good habit and never hurts you. Example disclosure you can adapt:

AI Use Disclosure: I used Claude (Anthropic) to help scope my research question, critique my methodology, and provide feedback on draft paragraphs. I verified every cited source myself using Google Scholar and my institution's library databases. All interpretations, conclusions, and any errors in this paper are my own.
For honors theses and senior capstones Talk to your advisor about AI use early, not the week before submission. Ask: "What's your policy on me using AI for (a) literature scanning, (b) methodology feedback, (c) code help, and (d) draft feedback?" Get the answer in writing. It protects both of you.
Try This Now Pick your current biggest research assignment. Run Step 1 above (the scoping interview) right now with Claude or ChatGPT. Come out of it with a research question that's more specific than the one you walked in with.

TL;DR

If you're a professor who landed here: this site is built to complement, not replace, your classroom AI policy. Use it, link it, or tell me to improve it.

Why this site exists

Most students are using AI. Most are using it badly. A small number of faculty have written thoughtful, detailed AI guidance for their own courses, but the average undergraduate is getting advice from TikTok and group chats.

This site gives them a decent default: a short guide to using AI as a scaffold for learning, with an explicit academic-integrity section that tells them to ask you first.
