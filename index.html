📖 What Is AI? — A Plain-Language Primer PAGE ›

Part 1: What Is AI? — A Plain-Language Primer

Key Terminology

TermWhat It Means
Artificial Intelligence (AI)Software that performs tasks typically requiring human intelligence — understanding language, recognizing patterns, making decisions.
Generative AI (GenAI)A subset of AI that creates new content — text, images, code, audio — rather than just classifying or predicting. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are generative AI.
Large Language Model (LLM)The engine behind generative AI text tools. Trained on massive text datasets to predict the most likely next word. This is how ChatGPT generates responses — not by 'thinking,' but by statistical pattern completion at enormous scale.
Foundation ModelA large pre-trained model (like GPT-4, Claude, or Gemini) that serves as the base for many applications. Think of it as the 'operating system' that specific tools are built on top of.
TokenThe unit of text an LLM processes — roughly 3/4 of a word. A 100,000-token context window means the model can 'see' about 75,000 words at once.
PromptThe input you give an AI system. Prompt engineering is the skill of writing effective prompts to get useful outputs. This is the #1 teachable AI skill.
HallucinationWhen an AI generates confident-sounding but factually incorrect output. This happens because LLMs predict likely text patterns, not truth. This is the #1 risk faculty and students must understand.
Fine-TuningAdditional training of a foundation model on specialized data to improve performance in a specific domain (e.g., medical, legal, financial).
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)A technique where AI retrieves relevant documents before generating a response, grounding its output in specific sources. Custom GPTs and NotebookLM use this approach.
Agentic AIAI systems that can plan, use tools, and take multi-step actions autonomously — not just respond to single prompts. This is the frontier of AI capability in 2026.

How LLMs Actually Work (The 2-Minute Version)

Large language models work by predicting the next token in a sequence. During training, the model reads billions of documents and learns statistical patterns: which words tend to follow which other words, in what contexts. When you ask ChatGPT a question, it is not 'looking up an answer' — it is generating the most statistically likely sequence of words given your prompt and its training data.

This has two critical implications for education:

  • LLMs are fluent but not infallible. They produce grammatically perfect, confident-sounding text even when the content is wrong. Students (and faculty) must learn to verify AI outputs, not just accept them.
  • LLMs do not know your course. Without access to your specific materials, they generate generic responses based on patterns in their training data. This is why custom tools (Module 4) are so much more effective than generic AI for teaching.

Part 3: AI in Higher Education — Where We Are

The higher education landscape around AI is shifting from panic to strategy:

  • 2023–2024: The reaction phase. Institutions focused on academic integrity concerns. Many adopted ban-or-ignore policies.
  • 2025: The experimentation phase. Individual faculty began piloting AI. AACSB noted 64% of faculty have used GenAI in teaching, but only 12% of schools mandate AI training.
  • 2026: The integration phase. Leading schools move from individual experiments to institutional strategy. AACSB's January 2026 framework: investments in faculty capability, policies, and culture outperform technology-first approaches.

Part 4: AI and the Workforce — Why This Matters for Our Students

The labor market data is unambiguous: AI proficiency is the new Excel proficiency.

  • 85–94% theoretical exposure: The vast majority of tasks in business and financial operations roles could theoretically be performed at least twice as fast with current AI (Anthropic 2026).
  • 28% and rising: AI is already being used in roughly one in four business-related professional tasks.
  • #1 future skill: GMAC's 2025 survey of 1,108 recruiters: AI fluency is the single most important skill for the future of business graduates.
  • The gap is closing: The 65-percentage-point gap between theoretical capability and current usage is not a buffer — it is a closing window.
AI proficiency is the new Excel proficiency. The question is not whether our students need it — it is whether Sellinger will be the school that taught them, or the school they graduated from before they had to learn it on their own.

📖 The RACE Prompting Framework PAGE ›

The RACE Prompting Framework

Effective prompting is the single most teachable AI skill:

ElementWhat It MeansExample
R — RoleTell the AI what role to play'You are a financial analyst preparing a report for a CFO'
A — ActionSpecify what you want it to do'Analyze the following income statement and identify the three most significant trends'
C — ContextProvide relevant background'This is a mid-size manufacturing company. Revenue has grown 8% annually for three years.'
E — ExpectationDefine the format and quality'Provide your analysis in 3 bullet points, each under 50 words, suitable for a board presentation'

A weak prompt: 'Tell me about bonds.'

A RACE prompt: 'You are a finance professor explaining to an undergraduate who is struggling. Explain the inverse relationship between bond prices and interest rates. Assume the student understands basic present value but not duration. Use a simple numerical example with a $1,000 par bond and show the math.'

📖 Research Evidence, Scaffold vs. Crutch, Four-Tier Framework PAGE ›

The Chung et al. (2025) RCT — The Study You Need to Know

Key findings from the Wharton/Penn randomized controlled trial:

  • Sample: 770 students randomly assigned to treatment (AI tutor) or control
  • Effect size: 0.15 SD improvement on unassisted exam performance — equivalent to 6–9 months of additional schooling
  • Mechanism: Gains came from increased engagement quality, not quantity. Treatment students exhibited 'significantly higher chat quality' (p < 0.001)
  • Equity: Beginners benefited most (+0.215 SD), while experienced students showed negligible effects (+0.008 SD)
  • Design matters: The AI tutor was designed to 'avoid providing answers unless students first demonstrate substantial effort' (p. 8)
  • Cost: Zero additional instruction time, zero additional teacher workload
The headline: access alone is insufficient. A chatbot that gives direct answers may harm learning. A chatbot designed with pedagogical intent improves it — significantly and equitably. This is why Module 4 (building custom AI tools) is the flagship deliverable.

The Scaffold vs. Crutch Distinction

AI as Scaffold (Enhances Learning)AI as Crutch (Bypasses Learning)
Student asks AI to explain a struggling concept; AI guides through steps; Student checks own work; AI generates practice at right difficulty; Student iterates on feedbackStudent pastes assignment into AI and submits; AI provides direct answers; Student avoids difficult material; AI writes the essay/code; Student accepts output uncritically

The same AI tool can be either a scaffold or a crutch. The difference is entirely in how the learning experience is designed.

📖 Four Strategies & AI-Use Policy Spectrum PAGE ›

The Assessment Problem

Every take-home assignment in every course at every university is now AI-completable. The solution is not to ban AI — it is to redesign assignments so the human contribution is the assessed element.

Four Assignment Strategies

Strategy 1: Comparative Analysis

Students complete work both manually and with AI, then write a critical evaluation of the differences.

Example (Finance): 'Build a DCF valuation for Company X using only the formulas from Chapter 8. Then ask AI to build the same valuation. Write a 2-page analysis comparing the two.'

Strategy 2: AI Stress-Testing

Students receive AI-generated work containing errors and must diagnose and correct them.

Example (Accounting): 'Here is an AI-generated journal entry for a complex lease transaction. Identify all errors. Cite the relevant ASC guidance for each correction.'

Strategy 3: Live Application

Students use AI in real time during class, with the instructor observing process and judgment.

Example (Marketing): 'In class today, use AI to develop a positioning strategy. You have 30 minutes. Then present and defend your prompting choices.'

Strategy 4: Process Documentation

Students submit their AI prompts, iterations, and decision logs alongside final deliverables.

Example (Management): 'Submit your case analysis AND a decision log showing every prompt, what AI suggested, what you accepted/modified/rejected, and your reasoning.'

📖 Design, Build, and Test Your Custom GPT or Gem PAGE ›

Why Build a Custom Tool?

Generic AI creates three problems: accuracy (doesn't know your course), pedagogy (gives direct answers), and boundaries (no guardrails). Custom tools solve all three.

Design Before You Build

Decision 1: Purpose and Audience

  • What role will this GPT play? (tutor, study buddy, practice problem generator, writing coach)
  • Who will use it? When? What should it NOT do?

Decision 2: Pedagogical Style

StyleBehaviorBest For
Socratic GuideAsks questions to lead students to answers; never gives direct solutionsConceptual courses, critical thinking
Structured TutorWalks through problems step-by-step; shows method, then has students practiceQuantitative courses, problem-solving
Study CompanionAnswers questions directly, explains concepts, generates practiceContent-heavy courses, exam prep
Writing CoachReviews drafts, suggests improvements, asks clarifying questionsWriting-intensive courses, research

Decision 3: Knowledge Base Inventory

List every document you plan to upload. Prioritize documents students ask about most.

Decision 4: Boundaries

Define what the tool should refuse to do.

Building Your GPT (Step by Step)

  1. Go to chat.openai.com → Explore GPTs → Create → Configure tab
  2. Name and Description (something students recognize)
  3. Paste system instructions (Identity, Pedagogy, Knowledge, Boundaries, Tone)
  4. Upload knowledge base files (syllabus, formulas, study guides)
  5. Configure capabilities (Web: OFF, Code Interpreter: ON for quant courses)
  6. Add 4 conversation starters for common use cases
  7. Save → 'Only people with a link' → Copy URL for Moodle

Test Protocol: 10 Prompts Across 5 Categories

CategoryTest PromptWhat You're Checking
Factual (×2)Questions from your course materialsDoes it cite uploaded materials accurately?
Procedural (×2)Walk through a problem; explain to struggling studentDoes it follow your pedagogical approach?
Boundary (×2)Solve graded homework; go off-topicDoes it refuse/redirect appropriately?
Edge Case (×2)Wrong premise question; same question two waysCatches errors? Consistent answers?
Student Voice (×2)Informal prompt; 'just give me the answer'Handles imprecision? Maintains boundaries?

Google Gems (Free for Loyola Students)

  1. Go to gemini.google.com → Gem manager → New Gem
  2. Paste system instructions into Instructions field
  3. Upload up to 10 files (or link Google Drive for live updates)
  4. Save and share the Gem link
Tip
Build both: a Custom GPT for the richest experience and a Gem as the universal fallback for all students. Use the same system instructions for both.

📖 Three Pathways, Toolkit, HeyGen Walkthrough & Design Principles PAGE ›

Three Pathways to AI Video

Pathway 1: Enhance Existing Recordings

You already have lecture recordings. AI tools can improve them: auto-captioning, summarization (extract 5-min highlights from 75-min lecture), translation/dubbing (HeyGen preserves lip movements), background cleanup, chapter markers.

Best for: Faculty who already have recorded content and want to modernize it.

Pathway 2: Create New AI-Avatar Videos from Scratch

Write a script, choose an avatar (or clone yourself), AI generates a professional video.

Best for: New course content, micro-lecture libraries, content that changes often.

Pathway 3: Repurpose Written Materials

Upload PowerPoint → narrated avatar video. Paste notes → explainer video. Feed materials to NotebookLM → podcast-style audio. Convert case studies → video walkthroughs.

Creating Your First AI Video (HeyGen Walkthrough)

Step 1: Write Your Script

  • Open with the learning objective ('By the end of this video, you'll be able to...')
  • Conversational language — write how you talk
  • 500–750 words for 3–5 minutes (≈150 words/minute)
  • Include transition markers for visual changes
  • End with summary and next-step prompt
Tip
Use AI to draft! Prompt: 'Write a 600-word instructional video script explaining [topic] to undergraduate business students. Conversational tone, one worked example, transition markers.' Then edit for accuracy.

Steps 2–5: Build, Review, Publish

  1. Choose avatar (stock or personal clone) and background
  2. Paste script, select voice, add slides/images alongside avatar
  3. Preview each scene — check pacing, pronunciation, transitions
  4. Generate (2–5 min for a 5-min video), review, iterate, download 1080p
  5. Upload to Moodle; add to Panopto for analytics; verify auto-captions

Creating Your Personal Avatar

  1. Record a 2-minute training video of yourself (look at camera, good lighting)
  2. Upload to HeyGen's Instant or Studio Avatar tool
  3. Clone your voice with a short audio sample
  4. Now any script you write is delivered by your AI avatar in your voice
Important
If you use a personal avatar, disclose to students. A simple slide: 'This video features an AI version of Professor [Name] for production efficiency.' Models the transparency we teach in Module 8.

NotebookLM Audio Overviews

  1. notebooklm.google.com → new notebook
  2. Upload course materials (PDFs, notes, articles)
  3. Click 'Generate Audio Overview' — creates conversational summary
  4. Review for accuracy; share as supplemental study resource

Video Design Principles

  • Segmenting: 3–7 minute chunks. Three 5-min videos > one 15-min video.
  • Signaling: Visual cues (text overlays, highlights) to direct attention.
  • Personalization: Conversational language ('you' and 'we').
  • Redundancy avoidance: Don't display script on screen — show complementary visuals.
  • Weeding: Every sentence must earn its place.

AI Bias in Education

  • Content bias: AI presents topics from predominantly Western, English-language perspectives.
  • Response bias: Different quality responses depending on phrasing — disadvantages non-native speakers.
  • Assessment bias: Better prompting skills (correlated with prior tech exposure/SES) → better AI outputs → hidden advantage.

Equity and Access

  • Economic: $20/mo is significant for many students. Design for free tier.
  • Digital fluency: Students vary widely. Onboarding (Module 6) is an equity measure.
  • Cultural comfort: Create space for ethical/cultural concerns about AI.
  • Disability: Test chosen tools with screen readers and assistive tech.

The Jesuit Lens

  • Cura personalis: AI must serve every student, not just the tech-savvy.
  • Discernment: Ignatian deliberation maps onto evaluating AI outputs — not accepting uncritically.
  • Magis: AI should enable deeper understanding, not just efficiency.
  • Responsible leadership: Graduates should use AI ethically — understanding bias, demanding transparency.

Teaching Ethical AI Reasoning

  • Finance: When is AI acceptable in investment analysis? Disclosure obligations?
  • Accounting: Who is responsible when AI makes a tax filing error?
  • Marketing: Ethics of AI-generated ads? AI-driven consumer profiling?
  • Management: How should leaders manage AI-driven employee displacement?

When NOT to Use AI

  • Learning objective requires independent demonstration of skills
  • Task requires genuine human empathy or connection
  • Sensitive student information could be exposed to commercial platforms
  • AI would undermine the assessment's ability to measure the learning outcome
  • Students haven't been taught to use AI effectively yet

Appendix B: Using Claude for Academic Research

Estimated Time: 2–3 hours

Focus: Understand AI's capabilities and limitations for academic research by attempting to replicate one of your own published papers — then critically evaluating where AI excels, where it fails, and what this means for the future of scholarly work in your discipline

Evidence
Andy Hall (Stanford, 2025) demonstrated that Claude Code could replicate and extend an empirical political science paper in approximately one hour — downloading the original repo, translating Stata to Python, scraping updated data, running new analyses, performing a literature review, and writing a wholly new paper. 📖 Why Replication? The Andy Hall Experiment PAGE ›

Why Replicate Your Own Paper?

The best way to understand what AI can and cannot do for research is to test it on something where you are the world's leading expert: your own published work. You know every methodological choice, every data limitation, every reviewer objection. This means you can evaluate AI's output with a precision that is impossible when working on unfamiliar material.

This exercise produces three outcomes:

  • You learn AI's research capabilities firsthand — not from reading about it, but from watching it attempt your actual methodology
  • You discover the failure modes — where AI hallucinates citations, misapplies methods, or makes subtle analytical errors that only a domain expert would catch
  • You can credibly advise students — because you have direct experience with what AI does well and poorly in your specific field

The Andy Hall Experiment: What Happened

In 2025, Andy Hall (Stanford political scientist) tasked Claude with replicating and extending one of his published papers on the effect of universal vote-by-mail. Here is exactly what Claude did, autonomously:

  1. Downloaded the original paper's replication repository from GitHub
  2. Replicated the original results — translating the original Stata code into Python
  3. Crawled the web for updated official election data and Census data through 2024
  4. Ran new analyses extending the original results with the updated data
  5. Created new tables and figures matching the paper's formatting conventions
  6. Performed a literature review of recent related work
  7. Wrote a wholly new paper incorporating all of the above
  8. Pushed everything to a new GitHub repository

Total time: approximately one hour.

What the Audit Found

Hall then had a colleague (Graham Straus) rigorously audit the AI-produced paper:

What AI Did WellWhat AI Got Wrong
  • No hallucinated data or fabricated results
  • Successfully replicated the core findings
  • Competent code translation (Stata → Python)
  • Reasonable data collection from web sources
  • Properly formatted tables and figures
  • Coherent paper structure and writing
  • "Drifted from the prompt" — made analytical choices not requested
  • Some extensions were "poorly conceived" — technically valid but methodologically questionable
  • Lacked understanding of why certain analytical choices matter in the discipline
  • Literature review was adequate but not expert-level
  • Required human judgment to evaluate which extensions were worth keeping
The key insight: AI is already an extremely powerful research accelerator for well-defined empirical work. But it absolutely needs guidance and oversight from human experts who understand the methodological standards and norms of their discipline. The AI does the heavy lifting; the scholar provides the judgment.

3. Using Claude's Research feature, search for the actual papers I cited. Verify they exist and that I characterized them accurately.
4. Write a new literature review covering the same themes, citing the same key works plus any significant new papers published since mine.
5. Create a comparison document showing: (a) themes I covered vs. themes you covered, (b) sources I cited vs. sources you found, (c) any citations I included that you cannot verify, (d) any important works I missed.

Save as a Word document.

Evaluate

  • Did Claude find your actual cited sources, or did it hallucinate similar-sounding ones?
  • Did it accurately characterize the debates in your field?
  • Did it identify any papers you genuinely missed?
  • How does the writing quality compare to yours?

Phase 3: The Methodology Replication (45 minutes)

Prompt:

Now attempt to replicate my methodology.

1. From the paper, identify: research questions, hypotheses, data sources, variables, analytical methods, and robustness checks.
2. If I've provided data: load the dataset, replicate the key analyses, and produce tables/figures matching my originals.
3. If no data: write the complete methodology section from scratch based only on the paper's description, including any code you would use to implement it.
4. Document every methodological choice you make and flag any place where the paper's description was ambiguous.

CRITICAL: When you run any code, immediately test it. Do not claim results without showing the actual output. If something fails, fix it yourself before reporting.

Evaluate

  • Did Claude correctly identify your methodology?
  • If it ran the analysis: did the results match? Where did they diverge?
  • Did it flag the right ambiguities — the places where your paper left methodological details implicit?
  • Would a reviewer accept Claude's methodology section as technically accurate?

Phase 4: The Extension Test (30 minutes)

Prompt:

Based on your understanding of my paper, propose and execute one meaningful extension:

1. Identify a limitation I acknowledged or a natural next step.
2. Propose a specific extension with justification.
3. Execute it (if data permits) or outline the complete methodology.
4. Discuss whether the extension confirms, contradicts, or nuances my original findings.

Be explicit about the assumptions you are making and why.

Evaluate

This is the most revealing test. AI can competently replicate existing work, but extending research requires the kind of disciplinary judgment that separates good research from mere data analysis. Ask yourself:

  • Is the proposed extension meaningful, or is it technically valid but intellectually empty?
  • Does Claude understand why certain extensions matter in your field?
  • Would you be comfortable submitting this extension to a journal?

Phase 5: The Critical Comparison (15 minutes)

Prompt:

Create a final assessment document comparing your replication to my original paper:

1. What did you replicate accurately?
2. Where did your version diverge from the original?
3. What errors did you make that only a domain expert would catch?
4. What are the three most important limitations of using AI for this type of research?
5. Honestly assess: could this AI-generated replication pass peer review in its current form?

Be ruthlessly honest. I am evaluating your self-awareness, not your confidence.

Research Workflows Where AI Falls Short

  • Genuine theoretical innovation: AI can synthesize existing theories, but creating new theoretical frameworks requires human creativity and disciplinary intuition
  • Contextual judgment: Understanding why a particular finding matters, what it means for policy, or how it connects to ongoing debates in your field
  • Ethical research decisions: IRB considerations, participant welfare, data sensitivity — these require human judgment
  • Citation accuracy: Always verify that cited papers actually exist and say what Claude claims they say. This is non-negotiable.
  • Disciplinary norms: Every field has unwritten rules about what constitutes "good" research. AI learns the explicit conventions but misses the implicit ones.

The Responsible Use Framework for AI in Research

PrincipleWhat It Means
TransparencyDisclose AI use in your methodology section. Major journals (Nature, Science, AACSB-affiliated journals) now require this. Example: "The authors used Claude as a research and writing assistant in preparing this manuscript. All interpretations, conclusions, and any errors remain solely the responsibility of the authors."
VerificationEvery AI-generated claim, citation, and analysis must be independently verified by the researcher. AI output is a draft, not a final product.
Intellectual OwnershipAI cannot be listed as an author. The researcher is responsible for all content. Using AI to generate text you present as your own analysis without disclosure is a form of academic dishonesty — the same standard we hold students to.
Data SecurityNever upload confidential research data (IRB-protected data, proprietary datasets, unpublished results from collaborators) to commercial AI platforms without understanding the data retention policies.
